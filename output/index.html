Tropical Weather Outlook

The National Hurricane Center (NHC) reports no new tropical cyclones are expected during the next 7 days.

Severe Weather Threats (5-Day Outlook)

Tennessee: Tennessee will experience a transition to a more active weather pattern this week, characterized by hot and humid conditions with increasing chances for daily showers and thunderstorms. While the overall severe weather threat remains low, these storms may produce localized flash flooding, frequent lightning, and strong wind gusts. Heat index values are expected to reach the upper 90s to low 100s across much of the state. ADVISORY: No active alerts or river flooding are reported at this time. All offices (OHX, MEG, MRX) confirm no active warnings.

Mississippi: Hot and humid conditions are the primary concern, with heat index values potentially reaching 105-110 degrees, likely prompting a Heat Advisory. ADVISORY: Rain and thunderstorm chances will increase starting Tuesday, which may help moderate temperatures later in the week. Some storms from Wednesday to Friday could become strong to isolated severe. No active alerts or river flooding are reported. Office JAN confirms no active warnings.

Alabama: A typical summer pattern is expected, with hot and humid conditions and daily chances for scattered afternoon thunderstorms. ADVISORY: These storms could produce gusty downburst winds, small hail, and heavy rainfall. Heat indices may approach 102-107 degrees but are expected to remain just below advisory criteria. A moderate rip current risk may develop for some coastal areas mid-week. No active alerts or widespread river flooding are expected. All offices (BMX, MOB, HUN) confirm no active warnings.

Georgia: A return to a characteristic summertime pattern brings daily afternoon thunderstorms, with increasing coverage through the week. ADVISORY: While widespread severe weather is not anticipated, isolated strong storms with gusty winds, heavy rain, and lightning are possible. Heat index values are a key concern, expected to reach 100-105 degrees. A moderate rip current risk is noted for the coast. No active alerts or river flooding are reported. All offices (FFC, JAX) confirm no active warnings.

Florida: A typical, diurnally-driven summer weather pattern will prevail, with daily showers and thunderstorms developing along sea breeze boundaries. ADVISORY: Primary storm threats include frequent lightning, gusty winds up to 50 mph, and heavy downpours. Oppressive humidity will lead to peak heat indices of 100-107 degrees in most afternoons. No widespread riverine flooding is expected. No active weather alerts are in effect for the state. All offices (TAE, TBW, MFL, MLB) confirm no active warnings.

North Carolina: In the wake of Tropical Depression Chantal's remnants, the state will return to a hot and humid summer pattern. ADVISORY: Heat indices may reach 105-109 degrees Tuesday, likely necessitating a Heat Advisory. Daily chances for scattered, strong-to-severe thunderstorms exist, with damaging downbursts and flash flooding as primary threats. A High Risk for Rip Currents advisory is in effect for all beaches. All offices (RAH, ILM, MHX) confirm an active rip current advisory.

South Carolina: A typical summer pattern will bring hot and humid conditions with daily diurnal thunderstorms. ADVISORY: These storms can produce pulse severe winds from wet microbursts, heavy rain, and lightning. Highs will be in the mid-to-upper 90s inland, with heat indices peaking between 100-108 degrees, which may approach Heat Advisory criteria. A moderate risk for rip currents is forecast for the Charleston County coast. All offices (CHS, GSP) confirm no active warnings.

U.S. Virgin Islands: Data was not available to generate a forecast for the U.S. Virgin Islands at this time.

Recommendations

  • Monitor local NWS office updates, especially regarding heat and storm developments.
  • Review flash flood safety protocols for areas with saturated ground.
  • Adhere to rip current warnings and stay out of the water on affected beaches.
  • Ensure hydration plans are in place for personnel exposed to extreme heat.
